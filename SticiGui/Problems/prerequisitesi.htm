Percentages

"Percent" literally means "per hundred," or "divided by 100." 100% is 100/100 = 1. 25% is 25/100 = 0.25. 72.1% is 72.1/100 = 0.721. 5482.3% is 5482.3/100 = 54.823. To convert a decimal number to a percentage, you just multiply by 100%. For example, 0.125 = 12.5%, and 19.4 = 1940%. To convert a fraction to a percentage, first convert it to a decimal, then multiply by 100%.

For example, 1½ = 1.5 = 150%, and 128/18 = 7.111 = 711.1%. To succeed in this class, you will need to be fluent in converting among fractions, percentages, and decimals.

Pay attention to whether an answer blank has a percent sign after it. If it does and you type a percent sign into the answer blank as well, your answer will be divided by 100 twice, not once. For example, 5% = 0.05, but 5%% = 0.0005.

Exponentiation

A number raised to an integer power p is the number times itself p times. For instance, x5 = x×x×x×x×x. A number raised to a negative power -r is the reciprocal of the number raised to the power r, whether or not r is an integer. In symbols, a-r = 1/ar. A number raised to a fractional power 1/q (where q is an integer) is the qth root of the number. Thus (a1/q)q = a. If p and q are integers, a number raised to the power p/q is the qth root of the number, raised to the power p: (ap/q) = (ap)1/q = (a1/q)p. A number raised to the power r, times the same number raised to the power s, is that number raised to the power r+s, whether or not r and s are integers: ar×as = ar+s.

Inequalities

x < y means that x is less than y. x > y means that x is greater than y. x ≤ y means that either x is less than y or x is equal to y; equivalently, it means that x is no larger than y. Similarly, x ≥ y means that x is no smaller than y.

Some students are uncomfortable with the fact that, for instance, 0≤1, because 0 cannot be equal to 1. Nonetheless, 0 is indeed less than or equal to 1— it is not greater than 1.

Roundoff Error

Roundoff error can destroy the accuracy of a calculation. Do not round off intermediate calculations in this course. This section illustrates why.

It is a mathematical fact (an identity) that for any list of numbers, the sum of the squares of the differences between the numbers and the mean of the list is exactly equal to the difference between the sum of the squares of the numbers, and the square of the average of the list times the length of the list. That is, if M is the average of the list, and there are n numbers in the list,

(first number - M )2 + (second number - M)2 + ... + (last number - M)2 =
(first number)2 + (second number)2 + ... + (last number)2 - n×M2.

Now calculate the sum of the squares of the entries in the original list, and subtract from it 5 times the square of the average the original list (5×1,000,000,0002). Use 10-13 digits of precision in your calculation (this is what a standard calculator would give).

Notice that these two mathematically equivalent ways of computing the same thing are not numerically equivalent, owing to roundoff error. The correct answer is the first one. If your calculator had an infinite number of digits, you would have gotten the same answer using the second method. The moral is always perform intermediate calculations using as many digits as you can. Do not round in the middle of a computation.
